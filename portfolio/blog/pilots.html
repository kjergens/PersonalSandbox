Image Is Everything: The Airline Pilot
Tue Jan 19, 2016 Tweet

Pilots used to be portrayed like this:

"When they played poker they played all night. When they smoked they smoked too much. When they made love the complaints were rare."

But that was bad news for plane manufacturers. Planes were originally small and sold to individuals, like a car. No one felt safe if it was the vehicle of these rugged broncos.

http://mentalfloss.com/article/52653/those-magnificent-ladies-their-flying-machines

Then they looked like this:

Aviatrix. There were more women pilots in the 1920s than today. Ladies Aeronautical Society. No finer past-time for a respectable young woman than to get out in the fresh air and exercise mind and body in the pursuit of the mechanodynamic sciences of flight!

Airplane companies wanted to sell the idea of planes are so safe and easy to use. These women look like they won't get dirty at all. It became a popular Hollywood character: the Aviatrix

This didn't work so well for commercial airlines. The Aviatrixes helped refine the activity of flying, but no one would pay big bucks to have Betty on her spring break from university fly you. If it was easy, its not an expensive service. If it's hard, Betty shouldn't be the pilot.

So they went through a very deliberate process of re-imaging the pilot. Professional (the business suit), trained (the naval cap and pins), a distant father figure (the intercom). Betty was now the co-pilot, then later the flight attendent.

It worked. People rated airline pilot as top profession until astronauts took over. The planes barely changed. Which is why this article is called Image is Everything.

With the enclosed cabin and improved technology, piloting became less dangerous, yet the airlines portraying the profession as more difficult.
