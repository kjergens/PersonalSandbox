If the Ancient Greeks are to be Believed...

Mon Jan 18

Back in school, if you had to read The Iliad you might have overlooked some funny passages regarding colors: the parts that say the sky is bronze, the sea is wine-colored, and the sheep are violet. Something got lost in translation, right?

Not necessarily. Most ancient texts, including the New Testament, use colors in ways you and I never would. Why is that? One theory is that different cultures actually see color differently. It seems to depends on your language and if a word exists for a particular color. If you don't have a word for blue - like how the the ancients didn't - your brain might not detect it, substituting in a color for which it does have a word!

Convincing evidence was videoed during the Himba Color Experment. A tribe in Namibia has no word for blue but several for green and the Himba volunteers couldn't pick out a blue swatch mixed in with a bunch of green ones.

It's not conclusive but very tempting to believe. Who wouldn't want to live in a word with violet sheep?
